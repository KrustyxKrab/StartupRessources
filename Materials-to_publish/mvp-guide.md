## Das MVP-Missverständnis

Most founders build the wrong MVP because they misunderstand what "minimum" means. They assume minimum means bad—a half-finished product bloated with bugs, something to be embarrassed about showing to users. This is precisely backward. An MVP is the smallest possible product that lets you test your riskiest hypothesis, and it doesn't have to do little; it has to do the right thing. Minimum doesn't mean minimal quality or minimal ambition; it means precisely calibrated scope. You're solving for one insight, not for market dominance. The entire point is to answer the question that, if answered wrong, kills your company—and then move to the next question.

## Typen von MVPs

There are four fundamental MVP types, each with different mechanics and revelatory power. The landing page MVP tests demand before anything is built at all—Dropbox famously started with a video explaining the product, and watched 75,000 signups pour in the first week, proving that the core problem was real and people wanted the solution. The landing page MVP teaches you whether people care, but it doesn't teach you if they'll actually use what you build or if your economics work. The concierge MVP inverts the process: you provide the service manually, personally, before you automate it. Zappos purchased shoes manually from local retailers, sent them to customers, and learned more about purchase behavior and fulfillment logistics in weeks than they could have guessed in months. The third type, the wizard of oz MVP, presents a fully functional-looking interface while a human operates everything behind the scenes—you test the frontend's usability and your own understanding of the workflow without engineering a backend. This is particularly useful when you're not sure if the user experience you've imagined is actually the experience users need. Finally, the prototype MVP uses clickable mockups in Figma or similar tools to test UX assumptions without writing code at all, letting you validate the interaction design before committing engineering resources.

## Die richtige Frage

Before you build anything, ask yourself: what is my riskiest assumption? Not "Can we build this?"—that's a technical question, and engineers can always build things given enough time and money. The question is "Do people actually want this?" If the answer is no, your engineering skill becomes irrelevant. Build the minimal possible experiment that answers this one question about your users, not about your technology. Once you've answered it, the next riskiest assumption surfaces, and you build the next minimum experiment. This discipline is what separates founders who learn from founders who burn runway.
