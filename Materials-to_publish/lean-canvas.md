## Was ist das Lean Canvas?

A traditional business plan takes months to write and is outdated before the ink dries. Ash Maurya's Lean Canvas solves a different problem: it forces brutal focus on the one thing that actually matters in a startup—whether customers want what you're building. Unlike the dense forecasts of classical business planning, which assume you can predict market behavior three years out, the Lean Canvas recognizes that startups thrive on learning, not on accuracy. You force yourself to articulate exactly which customers have which problems, and which solution assumptions are riskiest. The entire tool sits on one page because that constraint itself is pedagogical—it demands you kill every word that doesn't defend your core hypothesis.

## Die 9 Bausteine

**Das Fundament: Problem und Kundensegmente.** Your customer segment is never "everyone"—that is nobody. The segment must be so specific that you can walk into a coffee shop, name the person, describe their daily frustration, and predict how they'll react to your pitch. The problem section distills the customer's core pain into three specific problems, not because three is magical, but because three forces you to choose. The fatal mistake is building a product without this clarity: you end up with a solution hunting for a problem rather than a problem waiting for a solution.

**Die Lösung und ihr Versprechen.** The unique value proposition is the single hardest part of the canvas and also the most critical. It's not a tagline; it's the precise reason why your solution beats alternatives (including "doing nothing," which is the strongest competitor most startups face). The solution itself is only three features—the three capabilities that prove you've addressed the problem. Add more, and you've stopped building a minimum viable product and started building a product that dies before launch.

**Die wirtschaftliche Mechanik.** Revenue streams describe how money flows: per-user subscription, marketplace fees, one-time sales, freemium upsell. The cost structure captures the true economic reality—hosting, people, customer acquisition—not fantasy margins. Key metrics define what success actually looks like: if your metric isn't something you check weekly, it's not a key metric. Revenue, churn rate, customer acquisition cost, and retention curve all belong here because they're the variables you'll obsess over as you learn.

**Der unfaire Vorteil.** This is what cannot be copied within two years even if a competitor tries hard. It might be your personal network in a specific industry, exclusive access to data, a team skill that took years to develop, or regulatory approval that your competitors can't easily obtain. If you can't articulate what you have that competitors don't, your advantage is your execution speed—and that runs out within months.

## So füllst du es aus

Start with the problem, because the problem is your foundation; everything else builds from its clarity. Print the template or use Miro or FigJam—the medium matters less than the discipline of one page. Use sticky notes or editable blocks, not locked-in prose, because you will change this twenty times before you start building anything. Constrain each section to three points, which sounds arbitrary until you realize that the constraint itself forces thinking: you cannot list every possible problem, which means you have to choose the three that matter most. Validate every assumption—not with surveys, which lie, but with at least ten conversations with real potential customers who would actually buy from you. Each conversation should push you to revise the canvas; if you're not changing it based on what you learn, you're not learning.

The template itself is secondary. Download the official version from [leanstack.com](https://leanstack.com), or use the excellent templates available on Miro and FigJam. The tool is only as valuable as your willingness to iterate ruthlessly and your honesty about what customers actually tell you versus what you hope they're saying.
