## Was macht ein gutes Pitch Deck aus?

Investors see hundreds of decks, and they've internalized the pattern so thoroughly that deviations register instantly. The deck is not the pitch—it's the memory aid for the story you tell. The structure is known because it's been refined across thousands of successful capital raises; what's not known is your execution of it. Investors read defensively, not hopefully. They're not combing through your slides looking for reasons to say yes; they're looking for reasons to say no. Your deck must neutralize the most common objections before the Q&A even starts, and it must do it visually and verbally in the first three minutes. The slides are ammunition for the story, not the story itself.

## Die 12 Folien

**The opening act: Cover, Problem, Solution.** The cover slide needs nothing except your name, logo, and a single sentence that describes what you do. No more. The problem slide makes the problem tangible—use a short story, a concrete statistic, or a specific moment of customer frustration; avoid abstract descriptions of market pain. The solution slide shows how your product solves the problem, not how it works technically. This is the trickiest translation: a demo of your interface is not an explanation of your solution. A good solution slide explains the mechanism of relief—how the customer's life changes after using your product.

**Market and traction.** The market size slide establishes that you're chasing a problem worth solving at scale using TAM, SAM, and SOM, and it shows a realistic path forward where you could capture meaningful territory. The product slide is screenshots, demos, or mockups—show, don't tell. The traction slide is whatever you've achieved: paying customers, user growth, partnership logos, pilot outcomes, anything that proves the problem is real and people are willing to engage with your solution. This is your credibility slide; it answers the implicit question every investor asks: "Has anyone else validated this?"

**Economics and competition.** The business model slide answers "How do you make money?" with brutal simplicity—subscription per user, marketplace fee per transaction, one-time enterprise sale, whatever it is. The competition slide is not a brag about how you've crushed everyone else; it's an honest assessment of who your competitors are (including the "do nothing" option) and why your approach is different or better. Go-to-market articulates your path to your first 1,000 customers—not in abstract terms, but with specific channels and unit economics that prove you know how to acquire customers without spending yourself into bankruptcy.

**Foundation and ask.** The team slide answers the existential question: why are you and your cofounders the right people to solve this problem? Credential and background matter less than specific domain expertise or network in the problem space. The financials slide is a three-year projection—be realistic. Moonshot revenue curves trigger skepticism, not enthusiasm. The final slide is the ask: how much capital are you raising, and what will you use it for? Be specific. "Product development, team expansion, and customer acquisition" is better than "fuel growth." Investors want to understand how their money moves the needle.
