## Was ist Product-Market Fit?

Product-Market Fit is not a metric. It's a state—and you feel it before you can measure it. Marc Andreessen defined it as being in a good market with a product that can serve that market, which is true but circular. A clearer articulation: when you have PMF, people want your product so badly that you can't scale fast enough to meet demand. The friction that dominated your early months—convincing users to try you, explaining why they need you—evaporates. Users pull the product from you. Retention curves flatten at high levels. Your unit economics suddenly work. This transition from pushing to being pulled is so distinct that founders who've experienced it never forget the feeling.

## Das 40%-Kriterium

Sean Ellis, who popularized the term, developed a mechanical test: ask your users, "How would you feel if you could no longer use this product?" If 40% or more answer "very disappointed," you have Product-Market Fit. Below 40%, keep iterating. The threshold works mechanically because it captures something specific: willingness to actively miss the product, not passive preference. A user who answers "very disappointed" has integrated your product into their workflow; losing it creates friction in their day. This threshold separates products that are nice-to-have from products that are must-have. The test is ruthlessly simple because PMF itself is simple, even though the path to it is not.

## Weitere Signale

Product-Market Fit announces itself through multiple simultaneous patterns. Organic growth accelerates because users recommend you without prompting, and your customer acquisition becomes cheaper as word of mouth overpowers your paid channels—you're no longer shouting into the void. Churn flattens because customers actually stay; you're not running on a treadmill of activation followed by abandonment. Unit economics improve because higher retention and organic growth reduce your cost per acquisition relative to customer lifetime value. In the strongest cases, you develop a waitlist because demand exceeds supply; you're constrained by operations, not by demand generation. These signals rarely appear in isolation; they arrive together, and their synchronization is itself the signal.

## Wie du ihn findest

Product-Market Fit is always relative to a customer segment; chasing PMF across all possible customers is a path to mediocrity and burnout. Start by defining your target segment with specificity—not "small businesses" but "e-commerce companies doing $500k-$5M in annual revenue." Build a minimal but complete version of your product; not minimal in quality or minimal in features, but minimal in scope—it solves one problem end-to-end, not many problems halfway. Measure retention ruthlessly, because retention is the only honest signal of fit; activation metrics can be gamed, but retention cannot. Speak with your customers not to sell but to understand: what workflows did your product change, and did it change them in the way you predicted? With each conversation, iterate on either the product or the segment. If retention is low and customers aren't using the core feature you built, the product needs to change. If retention is reasonable but you're struggling to find more customers like your best customers, your segment definition was wrong. This cycle of learning, shipping, and measuring is the only reliable path to PMF.
